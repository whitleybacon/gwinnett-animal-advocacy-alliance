Lucy's Story

Lucy entered Gwinnett County Animal Shelter on May 21, 2025 after Animal Control responded to reports of an injured stray dog with a large mass between her hind legs. Despite her medical condition, she remained at the shelter for over two weeks before undergoing surgery. One week later, she was found with a catastrophic failure of her spay incision and exposed abdominal organs. She was euthanized due to the severity of her condition.

Medical Timeline

Lucy entered the shelter on May 21, 2025. Initial records documented a large mass between her hind legs, vomiting with bloody stool, and later the development of "happy tail." She underwent a spay procedure on June 7, 2025, during which the mass was also expected to be addressed.

On the morning of June 14, 2025, Lucy was found with complete dehiscence of her surgical incision, with abdominal contents protruding from the wound. Due to the severity of her injuries, she was humanely euthanized.

Documentation Concerns

The medical records contain no documented post-operative veterinary examinations during the seven days following Lucy's surgery. No incision checks, wound evaluations, or daily post-operative assessments appear in the records before the catastrophic surgical failure was discovered.

Medication records also show both pain medication (Carprofen) and antibiotics (Simplicef) prescribed following surgery, yet each medication is documented with "0 doses administered." Whether the medications were never given or simply never documented cannot be determined from the available records.

Why This Matters

Surgical complications can develop rapidly. Daily post-operative monitoring exists to identify swelling, pain, discharge, incision failure, infection, or other complications before they become life-threatening. Without documented follow-up, it is impossible to determine when Lucy's condition first began to deteriorate or whether earlier intervention could have prevented the catastrophic outcome.

Both the Planned PEThood contract and the County's current veterinary services contract require veterinarians to conduct daily rounds and evaluate animals needing medical attention. Yet Lucy's records contain no documented post-operative examinations between her June 7 surgery and the discovery of complete incision failure on June 14, raising important questions about whether required monitoring occurred and, if so, why it was not documented.
