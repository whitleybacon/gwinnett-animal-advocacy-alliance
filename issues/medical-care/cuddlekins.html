Cuddlekins

A mild respiratory illness. A rapid decline. Questions about monitoring and documentation.

Cuddlekins was found deceased in her kennel twelve days after entering the shelter.

Cuddlekins entered the shelter on August 12, 2024. Records later documented respiratory illness, lethargy, dehydration, weight loss, refusal to eat, and continued decline before she was found deceased on August 24.

What Happened

Cuddlekins entered Gwinnett County Animal Shelter on August 12, 2024, after being found abandoned outside Planned PEThood in Duluth. Shelter notes later described her as friendly, affectionate, and timid.

On August 19, veterinary notes documented mild clear nasal discharge and suspected a mild upper respiratory infection. Doxycycline was recommended.

By August 22, Cuddlekins’ condition had worsened. Records documented that she had not wanted to eat, was lethargic, did not want to stand, was approximately 7% dehydrated, and had experienced weight loss. The notes also stated that URI medications were prescribed on August 19 but had not been entered into the computer.

Cuddlekins was treated with fluids, Baytril, and appetite stimulation, and was moved off the adoption floor into veterinary care for better observation. Later that day, records stated she was sleeping in her kennel and did not appear to have eaten.

On August 23, records documented that Cuddlekins remained depressed, did not eat overnight, and immediately gagged when staff attempted to force feed her. She received additional fluids and medication.

On August 24, Cuddlekins was found deceased in her kennel.

Veterinary notes from August 22 state that medications for URI were prescribed on August 19 but were not entered into the computer. The medication section of the medical history report states, “No medications administered to this animal.”

August 22, 2024 Medical Decline

Condition Worsened

Cuddlekins was documented as lethargic, dehydrated, losing weight, not wanting to eat, and not wanting to stand. She received fluids, Baytril, and appetite stimulation and was moved into veterinary care for better observation.

Later that afternoon, records stated she was sleeping in her kennel and did not appear to have eaten. The plan was to repeat fluids and recheck the following morning.

August 23, 2024 Continued Decline

Still Not Eating

Cuddlekins remained depressed, did not eat overnight, and immediately gagged when force feeding was attempted. She received additional fluids and medication.

August 24, 2024 Outcome

Found Deceased

Cuddlekins was found deceased in her kennel.

The veterinary contract required daily rounds.

Both the Planned PEThood contract and the current A.W.A.G. veterinary contract require the veterinary team to perform daily rounds to check animals for signs of illness or injury, review care requests throughout the day, and maintain medical records.

Cuddlekins’ records documented a progression from respiratory symptoms to lethargy, dehydration, weight loss, refusal to eat, inability or unwillingness to stand, and death within several days.

If daily veterinary rounds were being performed as required by contract, why did Cuddlekins’ condition continue to deteriorate until she was found deceased in her kennel?

Why This Case Matters

Upper respiratory infections are common in shelter environments, but they require monitoring because illness can progress quickly, especially when an animal stops eating or drinking. When a cat becomes lethargic, dehydrated, loses weight, and refuses food, timely intervention and close observation become critical.

Cuddlekins’ case raises concerns about illness recognition, medication documentation, daily monitoring, and continuity of care. The records show that URI medication was recommended, but later notes state the medication was not entered into the computer, while the medication section states no medications were administered.

When medical documentation is incomplete or inconsistent, it becomes difficult to determine what care was actually provided, whether treatment was timely, and whether earlier intervention could have changed the outcome.
