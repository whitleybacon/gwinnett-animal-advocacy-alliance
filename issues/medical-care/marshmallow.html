Marshmallow

A routine surgery. A severe infection. Questions that deserve answers.

No documented veterinary checks were recorded during the seven days following Marshmallow's surgery.

Marshmallow was neutered on July 1, 2025. The first documented care requests raising concern were entered on July 8, after staff noted he was not eating, lethargic, bleeding, swollen, and unable to walk without falling over.

What Happened

Marshmallow entered Gwinnett County Animal Shelter on June 26, 2025, after being found at Lions Club Park in Lilburn. According to shelter records, he had reportedly been living around the park for several months before being impounded. Intake notes described him as friendly and social.

On July 1, 2025, Marshmallow underwent a routine neuter procedure at the shelter. While the surgery itself was documented, the medical record contains no documented veterinary rechecks or post-operative examinations during the seven days that followed.

On July 8, shelter staff submitted multiple veterinary care requests after observing significant changes in Marshmallow's condition. Records note that he had stopped eating, appeared lethargic, was bleeding from the surgical site, had severe swelling, appeared dehydrated, and was unable to walk without falling over.

When Marshmallow was examined later that day, veterinary records described extensive swelling, gas beneath the skin, foul-smelling drainage, and a severe post-operative infection. His prognosis was documented as guarded.

Although treatment was initiated, Marshmallow's condition continued to deteriorate over the following several days. Medical records documented ongoing pain, worsening tissue damage, and continued decline. On July 12, 2025, Marshmallow was humanely euthanized due to the severity of his condition.

Because the medical record contains no documented post-operative veterinary examinations between July 1 and July 8, questions remain regarding when Marshmallow's condition began to deteriorate, whether earlier intervention may have changed the outcome, and whether post-operative monitoring and documentation met expected standards of care.

Why This Case Matters

Routine surgeries require careful post-operative monitoring because complications can develop gradually over time. Animals cannot communicate pain or worsening medical conditions, making regular observation and documentation critical to ensuring timely intervention.

Marshmallow's case raises broader questions about medical oversight, post-operative care, documentation, and accountability for animals receiving veterinary treatment while housed at the shelter.

The veterinary contract required daily rounds.

Both the Planned PEThood contract and the current A.W.A.G. veterinary contract require the veterinary team to perform daily rounds to check animals for signs of illness or injury, review care requests throughout the day, and maintain medical records.

Marshmallow underwent surgery on July 1, 2025, yet the medical record contains no documented veterinary recheck for the following seven days. By July 8, he was documented as not eating, lethargic, bleeding, severely swollen, dehydrated, unable to walk normally, and suffering from a severe post-operative infection.

If daily veterinary rounds were being performed as required by the contract, why was Marshmallow's deteriorating condition not documented or addressed sooner?

Why This Case Matters

Routine surgeries require careful post-operative monitoring because complications can develop gradually over time. Animals cannot communicate pain, swelling, infection, or worsening medical distress, making daily observation, documentation, and timely follow-up essential.

Marshmallow's case raises broader questions about whether contractual requirements for daily veterinary rounds, post-operative monitoring, medical documentation, and follow-up care were consistently carried out in practice. When medical records do not reflect the monitoring required by contract, it becomes difficult to determine when an animal's condition began to decline or whether earlier intervention could have changed the outcome.
