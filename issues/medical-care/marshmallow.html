Marshmallow

A routine surgery. A severe infection. Questions that deserve answers.

No documented veterinary checks were recorded during the seven days following Marshmallow's surgery.

Marshmallow was neutered on July 1, 2025. The first documented care requests raising concern were entered on July 8, after staff noted he was not eating, lethargic, bleeding, swollen, and unable to walk without falling over.

What Happened

Marshmallow was a white Labrador mix who entered Gwinnett County Animal Shelter on June 26, 2025, after being found at Lions Club Park in Lilburn. Records indicate he had reportedly been loose at the park for months before being impounded.

On July 1, 2025, Marshmallow was neutered at the shelter. His medical records note the procedure as a routine castration, with no medical notes stored before surgery and no documented post-surgical exam completed in the record.

On July 8, staff submitted care requests noting that Marshmallow had not eaten since the previous evening, was lethargic, bleeding, swollen, dehydrated, and unable to walk without falling over. Later that day, veterinary notes described a severe infection with swelling, gas under the skin, foul-smelling fluid, and a guarded prognosis.

Despite treatment after the infection was identified, Marshmallow continued to decline. On July 12, 2025, he was euthanized after continued refusal to eat, swelling, elevated respiratory rate, and worsening tissue damage.

What the Records Show

Medical Timeline

Marshmallow entered the shelter on June 26, 2025. He was neutered on July 1, 2025. The first documented care requests raising concern were entered on July 8, when staff noted decreased appetite, lethargy, bleeding, swelling, possible dehydration, and difficulty walking.

Veterinary notes from July 8 described extensive swelling, gas under the skin, foul-smelling fluid, and suspected post-operative infection. His prognosis was described as guarded due to the severity of the infection. He was euthanized on July 12, 2025.

Documentation Concerns

The records show Marshmallow received surgery on July 1, but no documented veterinary recheck appears in the record during the seven days following surgery. Medication records also show pain management and GI medication listed with “0” doses administered.

Because animals cannot communicate pain or medical decline, post-operative monitoring and accurate documentation are essential. Without documented follow-up, it is difficult to determine when Marshmallow began declining or whether earlier intervention could have changed the outcome.

Why This Matters

Marshmallow’s case illustrates why routine surgeries still require consistent monitoring. Post-operative complications can develop over time, and early signs such as swelling, pain, bleeding, appetite changes, or lethargy should trigger prompt review.

This case raises broader concerns about medical oversight, post-operative care, documentation, and accountability for animals housed in County custody.
