Medical Care

Every animal entering Gwinnett County Animal Shelter depends on the shelter for medical care, monitoring, and treatment.

Public records reviewed by Gwinnett Animal Advocacy Alliance identify recurring concerns involving delayed care, incomplete documentation, post-operative monitoring, and accountability.

When animals cannot speak, medical oversight matters.

Medical records reviewed through Open Records Requests identified recurring patterns involving delayed treatment, gaps in medication documentation, missed monitoring, and animals whose conditions significantly worsened before intervention occurred.

Common Findings

The reviewed records point to recurring issues involving documentation, monitoring, escalation, and oversight.

Medication Documentation Gaps

Accurate medical records are essential to ensuring animals receive appropriate treatment and that care can be verified. A review of Open Records Requests identified recurring instances where medications were prescribed or referenced in treatment plans, yet medical records reflected “0 doses administered” or lacked documentation confirming treatment occurred. When medication administration cannot be verified, continuity of care suffers, accountability is weakened, and it becomes impossible to determine whether prescribed care was actually provided.

Failure to Monitor Medical Decline

Animals recovering from illness or surgery depend entirely on shelter staff to recognize changes in their condition. The medical records reviewed identified repeated instances where animals showed signs of illness or decline over several days before documented veterinary reassessment occurred. In several cases, animals were not examined until their condition had become significantly more severe, reducing opportunities for earlier intervention and treatment.

Post-Operative Monitoring Concerns

Routine spay and neuter surgeries still require careful follow-up. The reviewed case records identified concerns involving missing or incomplete post-operative checks, lack of documented incision monitoring, and delayed recognition of surgical complications. Post-operative monitoring exists because complications may develop over time, and early detection can be the difference between recovery and preventable suffering.

Delayed Diagnosis of Serious Conditions

Several animals displayed symptoms that may indicate serious illness or injury, including weakness, neurologic signs, trauma, infection, and severe lethargy. In some cases, the full severity of an animal’s condition became apparent only after transfer to rescue or outside veterinary care. This raises concerns about whether medical conditions were being recognized, escalated, and treated with appropriate urgency while animals remained in County custody.

Disease Prevention and Sanitation Concerns

High-volume shelters must rely on strong disease prevention, vaccination, sanitation, isolation, and monitoring practices to protect vulnerable animals. The report identified concerns involving kitten illness, disease outbreaks, unassisted deaths, and environmental sanitation. These issues are especially serious for young kittens, sick animals, and animals with limited immune protection.

Veterinary Oversight and Accountability

Medical care in a public shelter must be timely, documented, and accountable. The reviewed records raise concerns about whether the existing veterinary care model provided enough daily oversight, consistent follow-up, medication verification, and clear escalation pathways. Without strong oversight, the County cannot reliably verify that animals received the care they needed when they needed it.

Why This Matters

For Animal Welfare

Timely treatment and monitoring can prevent suffering and save lives.

For Taxpayers

Delayed care can lead to more costly emergencies and greater County liability.

For Rescue Partners

Rescues often face critical conditions that may have been preventable.

For Public Confidence

Transparency and accountability build trust in how animals are cared for.

What Should Change

Gwinnett County needs a medical care system that is timely, documented, transparent, and accountable.

Consistent Daily Medical Rounds and Post-Operative Monitoring

Animals recovering from surgery, illness, injury, or medical treatment should receive consistent daily observation and documented follow-up. Post-operative checks should include incision monitoring, pain assessment, and prompt escalation when complications appear.

Standardized Medication Documentation

Medication records should clearly show what was prescribed, when it was administered, who administered it, and whether the animal completed the treatment plan. If medication administration cannot be verified, the County cannot confirm that care was delivered.

Stronger Disease Prevention and Sanitation Practices

The shelter should use consistent vaccination, isolation, cleaning, disinfection, and outbreak response protocols to protect vulnerable animals and reduce preventable illness.

Clear Escalation Pathways and Veterinary Availability

Kennel staff, volunteers, and rescue coordinators need clear, responsive pathways for escalating medical concerns when an animal appears sick, injured, declining, or in distress.

Transparent Records and Independent Oversight

Medical records, medication logs, care requests, and outcomes should be complete, reviewable, and subject to meaningful oversight. Public confidence depends on the County being able to verify the care animals receive.

Every Animal Depends on Someone Speaking Up

When an animal enters the shelter, it cannot request medical care, ask for a second opinion, or report that something is wrong. Strong oversight, transparency, and accountability are essential.
