Camera Coverage & Accountability

No cameras in animal housing areas, medical treatment areas, or euthanasia areas.

Critical areas are not currently covered by cameras.

Animal housing areas, medical treatment areas, and euthanasia areas currently lack camera coverage, leaving some of the most sensitive shelter operations without objective video documentation.

When Questions Arise, The Truth Matters

Over the years, questions have arisen about animal handling, kennel operations, staff interactions, volunteer concerns, and incidents involving animals at Gwinnett Animal Shelter. In many of these situations, there was no objective video documentation available.

Instead, investigators, shelter leadership, volunteers, and the public have often been left relying on:

  • Conflicting witness statements
  • Written recollections
  • Interviews conducted weeks later
  • Photographs taken after the fact
  • Incomplete documentation

Objective camera footage cannot answer every question—but it can answer many of them.

When questions arise, decisions affecting animals, employees, volunteers, and the public should be based on objective evidence whenever possible, not conflicting recollections after the fact.sure decisions are based on evidence rather than conflicting recollections.

The April 2026 Camera Proposal

In April 2026, the Gwinnett Animal Advisory Council discussed expanding camera coverage inside Gwinnett Animal Shelter after a conversation about an injured dog and the difficulty of determining exactly what had occurred. A council member proposed exploring additional cameras in animal housing areas to improve documentation, transparency, and accountability when incidents happen.

The proposal received support from several council members, who argued that even partial camera coverage would provide valuable documentation and help answer questions when incidents occur. Others questioned the effectiveness of additional cameras, whether they would adequately capture animal areas, and whether funding would be available.

Rather than directing staff to evaluate implementation options, develop cost estimates, or determine how expanded camera coverage could improve accountability, the proposal was ultimately tabled.

During the discussion, Animal Welfare Director Katrina Helman stated twice that "the county has no interest in adding additional cameras." Because the Animal Advisory Council meets quarterly, tabling the proposal delayed any further discussion for several months while shelter renovations continued.

Why It Matters

Documents Incidents

Cameras provide objective documentation of events within the shelter when questions or concerns arise.

Protects Animals, Staff, Volunteers & The Public

Cameras can help ensure safety, support fair investigations, and reduce disputes about what occurred.

Builds Public Trust

Transparency strengthens confidence in shelter operations and shows a commitment to accountability.

The Solution

Gwinnett County should add camera coverage in animal housing, medical treatment, and euthanasia areas — or at minimum, formally evaluate cost, feasibility, and implementation options before dismissing the proposal.

Transparency Should Not Be Controversial.

Cameras do not replace trust. They support accountability.
