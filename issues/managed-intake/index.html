Managed Intake

When shelter intake is restricted, animals and residents are often left without immediate assistance.

Understanding the impact is the first step toward finding humane, practical solutions.

Intake Access Concern
Key Concern

Managed intake shifts the burden onto residents, rescuers, and the animals themselves.

When a public shelter limits intake or cannot respond to stray and unwanted animals, community members are often left trying to manage urgent animal welfare situations on their own.

The Issue

Managed intake is often presented as a way to reduce overcrowding and improve shelter outcomes. But when intake restrictions are used without enough community support, enforcement, field response, spay/neuter access, rescue coordination, and transparent decision-making, animals can be left in unsafe or unstable situations.

In Gwinnett County, advocates and residents have repeatedly raised concerns about stray animals not being picked up, owner surrender requests being denied, and residents being left without clear solutions when they cannot safely keep or manage an animal.

Real-World Impact

Stray Animals Remain Outside

When Animal Control cannot respond or intake is restricted, stray animals may remain loose, injured, scared, or at risk of being hit by cars.

Residents Are Left Responsible

Finders and pet owners may be told to hold animals themselves, even when they do not have the space, resources, or ability to do so safely.

Rescues Carry More Burden

Rescue groups and individual advocates are often expected to absorb the consequences of limited public shelter access.

Animals Lose Time

Delays in intake, evaluation, medical care, networking, and placement can make outcomes worse for animals who need help quickly.

Why This Matters

A public animal shelter is a critical safety net. When that safety net becomes difficult to access, the consequences do not disappear — they move into neighborhoods, roads, social media groups, private homes, and already overwhelmed rescue networks.

Managed intake cannot work as a stand-alone policy. It must be paired with prevention, field response, community resources, enforcement, transparent data, and enough shelter capacity to respond to urgent animal welfare needs.

What We Are Advocating For

Clear Intake Policies

Residents should understand when animals will be accepted, when Animal Control will respond, and what options are available when the shelter cannot take an animal immediately.

Urgent Response Pathways

Injured, aggressive, abandoned, or at-risk animals need clear escalation options so urgent cases do not fall through the cracks.

Transparent Data

The county should publicly report intake requests, accepted intakes, denials, field response limits, outcomes, and reasons animals are turned away.

Long-Term Solutions

Expand Spay/Neuter Access

Prevention is essential. Affordable and accessible spay/neuter programs help reduce the number of unwanted litters and future shelter demand.

Strengthen Enforcement

Owner accountability, abandonment enforcement, and backyard breeding concerns must be addressed if the county wants to reduce intake pressure.

Support Community Help

Pet retention resources, food support, veterinary assistance, foster support, and rescue partnerships can help prevent unnecessary shelter intake.

Animals Should Not Be Left Without Help

Managed intake should not mean managed abandonment. Gwinnett County needs a humane, transparent, and accountable system that protects animals while supporting residents and rescue partners.
