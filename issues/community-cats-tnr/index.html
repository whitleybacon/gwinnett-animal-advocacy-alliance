Community Cats & TNVR

A humane, evidence-based approach that improves animal welfare, reduces shelter intake, and supports healthier neighborhoods.

Explore Gwinnett's Program

What Are Community Cats?

Community cats are unowned, free-roaming outdoor cats that may be friendly, unsocialized, or feral. They often live in family groups called colonies and may rely on community caregivers for food, shelter, safety, and medical support.

The Local Need

An estimated 50,000–70,000 community cats may live in Gwinnett County.

Without effective intervention, unsterilized cats continue reproducing, increasing the number of kittens born outdoors and placing additional pressure on shelters, rescue groups, caregivers, and neighborhoods.

How TNVR Works

Trap-Neuter-Vaccinate-Return (TNVR) is a humane, evidence-based approach to managing community cat populations while preventing future litters and improving animal welfare.

1

Trap

Cats are safely and humanely captured using appropriate live traps.

2

Neuter

Each cat receives spay or neuter surgery to prevent future litters.

3

Vaccinate

Cats receive important vaccinations, typically including rabies protection.

4

Return

After recovery, cats are returned to their familiar outdoor territory.

Benefits of TNVR

TNVR benefits animals, residents, taxpayers, neighborhoods, and the shelter system by preventing future litters before they occur.

Population Control

Sterilization prevents future litters and helps stabilize outdoor cat populations over time.

Saves Animal Lives

Preventing unwanted litters reduces kitten suffering and improves the health and welfare of existing colonies.

Reduces Shelter Strain

Keeping healthy community cats out of shelters preserves limited space and resources for animals requiring intervention.

Reduces Public Costs

Preventing shelter admissions can reduce spending on housing, feeding, staffing, and medical care.

Supports Public Health

Rabies vaccination helps protect cats, residents, pets, and the wider community.

Improves Coexistence

Sterilization reduces mating behaviors such as roaming, fighting, spraying, and excessive noise.

Why Prevention Matters

Community cat overpopulation has serious consequences for kittens, shelters, rescue organizations, taxpayers, and the animals themselves.

Outdoor Kitten Survival

75% Estimated mortality rate for kittens born in outdoor colonies

Many die from preventable causes or treatable illnesses, including upper respiratory infections.

Where Kittens Come From

80% Estimated share of kittens born to unsterilized community cats

Expanding TNVR is one of the most effective ways to prevent future litters and reduce suffering.

Shelter Impact

Sheltering Community Cats Is Expensive.

In 2025, Gwinnett County Animal Shelter admitted 2,492 cats. Of those, 1,740 were adopted, 63 were reunited with their owners, 251 were transferred to rescue organizations, and 337 cats died or were euthanized.

At an estimated average cost of approximately $750 per animal, caring for these cats represented nearly $2 million in shelter-related expenditures. Expanding Trap-Neuter-Vaccinate-Return (TNVR) helps prevent future litters, reduces unnecessary shelter admissions, and allows limited shelter resources to be focused on animals that truly require intervention.

Gwinnett County's Community Cat Program

Gwinnett County offers free TNVR services for eligible community cats through the Bill Atkinson Animal Welfare Center. Appointments are offered on weekdays on a first-come, first-served basis, with limits on the number of cats accepted each day and per household.

Opportunities to Strengthen the Program

Gwinnett County already has an important foundation in place. Practical improvements could make the program easier to use and help more community cats receive timely services.

Expand Scheduling Options

Additional scheduling flexibility or alternative drop-off arrangements could improve access for working residents.

Increase Public Education

Coordinated outreach could help residents understand TNVR, locate available services, and learn how to participate.

Support Community Caregivers

Caregivers and volunteers need clear guidance, reliable communication, and practical support to manage colonies successfully.

Increase Access to Humane Traps

Expanded trap-loan programs could remove one of the most common barriers to participating in TNVR.

Accommodate Larger Colonies

Flexible or higher-capacity appointments could help residents working with larger colonies address them more efficiently.

Expand Volunteer Partnerships

Partnerships with experienced trappers, caregivers, rescues, and volunteers could increase the program's reach and effectiveness.

Focus Eligibility on Where the Cats Live

Some experienced volunteers live outside Gwinnett but trap cats located inside the county. Exploring ways to work with them could help more Gwinnett cats receive services.

Track and Share Outcomes

Regular reporting on available appointments, utilization, neighborhoods served, and program outcomes can help guide future improvements.

Why This Matters

Preventing future litters is one of the most effective ways to reduce suffering.

Unsterilized community cats account for a substantial share of kittens born each year. Many outdoor kittens do not survive, while others enter shelters or require intervention from already stretched rescue organizations and volunteers. Humane prevention benefits cats, residents, neighborhoods, and the county as a whole.

How You Can Help

Community cat management depends on informed residents, dedicated caregivers, volunteers, rescue partners, and accessible public services.

  • Learn how TNVR works and share accurate information.
  • Help community caregivers access humane traps and transportation.
  • Volunteer with a local community cat or rescue organization.
  • Share information about Gwinnett County's available program.
  • Support responsible colony care, vaccination, and monitoring.
  • Advocate for accessible, well-supported TNVR resources.
