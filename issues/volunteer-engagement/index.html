Volunteer Engagement

Volunteers are one of the most valuable resources available to any animal shelter. They provide enrichment, exercise, adoption support, photography, networking, rescue outreach, and thousands of hours of unpaid labor that directly benefit animals. When volunteer programs thrive, animals benefit. When volunteers disengage, animals lose critical support.

The Data

Gwinnett lags far behind neighboring counties.

Public records obtained by advocates showed Gwinnett Animal Shelter logged only 2,262 volunteer hours in 2025. By comparison, DeKalb County logged more than 18,000 volunteer hours, Fulton County nearly 13,000, and Hall County more than 4,800 hours despite having a much smaller population.

Why Volunteer Engagement Matters

Animal Enrichment

Volunteers provide walks, playtime, socialization, and mental stimulation that help reduce kennel stress and improve quality of life.

Adoption Visibility

Photos, videos, and networking help animals reach potential adopters and rescue partners.

Community Support

Volunteers serve as ambassadors for the shelter and strengthen public trust and community involvement.

Challenges Reported by Volunteers

Advocates and former volunteers have identified several barriers that may be contributing to low participation and retention.

Long Wait Times

Residents have reported waiting months between applying, attending orientation, completing shadow shifts, and becoming active volunteers.

Participation Restrictions

Programs such as Dog Day Out have historically required significant volunteer hours before participation is allowed.

Photo & Video Restrictions

Restrictions on photography and video have limited volunteers' ability to help promote adoptable animals.

Communication Concerns

Some volunteers have reported feeling discouraged from raising concerns or providing feedback about shelter operations.

The Retention Cliff

Analysis of volunteer records showed that many new volunteers logged only a few hours before leaving the program. Advocates estimate that approximately 68% of new volunteers recorded fewer than five hours before disengaging.

While some turnover is expected in any volunteer program, a retention rate this low raises important questions about volunteer recruitment, onboarding, support, communication, and program design.

Every volunteer who leaves represents lost opportunities for enrichment, networking, adoption promotion, and lifesaving support for shelter animals.

What We Are Advocating For

Improve Access

Reduce barriers to entry and create more opportunities for community members to become involved.

Support Collaboration

Encourage constructive communication between staff, volunteers, rescues, and community stakeholders.

Increase Transparency

Share volunteer participation data and program outcomes so the community can help identify solutions.

Volunteers Should Be Partners in Lifesaving

A successful shelter depends on strong community engagement. Gwinnett County has an opportunity to build a volunteer program that welcomes participation, supports transparency, and maximizes positive outcomes for animals.
